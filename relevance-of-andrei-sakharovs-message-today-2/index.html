Relevance of Andrei Sakharov's Message Today

Not too long ago, May 21, 2021 marked the 100th anniversary of the birth of Andrei Sakharov, the father of the Soviet hydrogen bomb and a staunch defender of human rights. His centenary arrived during a significant moment of reflection on many recent global events and discussions about the state of human rights around the world.

Who was Andrei Sakharov?

Having been born to a family of Dmitrii Sakharov, a physics professor, and Yekaterina Sakharova (Sofiano), a daughter of an army general, Andrei Sakharov found his calling first in the development of thermonuclear weapons and then as a political activist concerned with nuclear nonproliferation, peace, and civil liberties. It is this latter work that earned him The Nobel Peace Prize in 1975 "for his struggle for human rights in the Soviet Union, for disarmament and cooperation between all nations." 

Contribution to Soviet Nuclear History

Beginning with his student days at the Moscow State University, Sakharov was believed to be one of the most brilliant students in the history of the physics department. Following the end of his studies, Sakharov began to work at the prestigious Lebedev Institute of Physics of the Soviet Academy of Science under the future Nobel laureate Igor Tamm. 

As a young scientist, Sakharov was published twice in the Soviet Journal of Experimental and Theoretical Physics with his research concerning the cosmic rays. He then began work on the H-bomb which was first tested as RDS-37 in 1955 and subsequently as what became known as Tsar Bomba in 1961.

Political Activism

While at first Sakharov believed in the "vital importance of creating a Soviet super-weapon -- for our country [the Soviet Union] and for the balance of power throughout the world," it is around this time that his concerns around the dangers of nuclear weapons began to emerge. In 1973, The New York Times' Hedrick Smith quoted Sakharov:

I gradually began to understand the criminal nature not only of nuclear tests, but of the enterprise, as a whole. I began to look on it and on other world problems from a broader, human perspective.

Sakharov tried his best to convince General Secretary Khrushchev in 1958 to stop large-scale nuclear tests, but, alas, he did not succeed. Three years later, Sakharov tried again with a note to General Secretary Khrushchev that said: "to resume tests after a three-year moratorium would undermine the talks on banning tests and on disarmament, and would lead to a new round in the armaments race -- especially in the sphere of intercontinental missiles and anti-missile defense." General Secretary remained unconvinced. Feeling responsible for the lives impacted and lost due to nuclear test explosions as well as for the environmental damage, Sakharov fought earnestly until the 1963 Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty to which the U.S., the U.K., and the Soviet Union signed on after many years of negotiation. 

It was terrible. I had an awful sense of powerlessness. I could not stop something I knew was wrong and unnecessary. After that, I felt myself another man. I broke with my surroundings. It was a basic break. After that, I understood there was no point in arguing.

Sakharov, however, remained dedicated to various social causes and in June 1968 (following Prague Spring and Soviet invasion into Czechoslovakia), he published his first book Progress, Coexistence, and Intellectual Freedom, in which he discussed the dangers of thermonuclear war, hunger, poverty, freedom of thought and expression, pollution, and outlined steps for potential cooperation between the U.S. and the Soviet Union.

Following the publication of the book, Sakharov was removed from secret state projects in August 1968. 

Having been present at political trials of Soviet dissidents, Sakharov concluded that "what we [the Soviet Union] need is the systematic defense of human rights and ideals and not a political struggle, which would inevitably incite people to violence, sectarianism, and frenzy."

The next publication was a 1975 book called My Country and the World, the same year that Sakharov received the Nobel Peace Prize which his wife Yelena Bonner accepted on his behalf.

Throughout Sakharov's career as a humanist, he was not treated kindly by the Soviet Union. Confrontational relationship culminated in Sakharov and his wife being detained and exiled to Gorky (Nizhnii Novgorod) for 6 years during which Sakharov went on prolonged hunger strikes three times. 

Finally, in 1988, Sakharov was able to go on his first trip abroad to the West and meet with the U.S. Presidents Reagan and Bush, French President Mitterrand, and British Prime Minister Thatcher. 

Legacy

While today we longer find ourselves living during the Cold War, we are still grappling with many of the same questions that Andrei Sakharov brought up in his writings, speeches, and interviews. His parting words in My Country and the World where he discusses his global realities echo much of what activists say today about the present state of global affairs:

"The reality of the contemporary world is complex, with many planes. It is a fantastic mix of tragedy, irreparable misfortune, apathy, prejudices, and ignorance, plus dynamism, selflessness, hope, and intelligence. The future may be even more tragic. Or it may be more worthy of human beings -- better and more intelligent. Or, again, it may not be at all. It depends on all of us -- people living in the USSR, China, India, the United States, Egypt, Israel, Thailand, Italy, England, France, Japan, Australia, Holland, Germany, Syria, Vietnam, Iran -- in every country in the world. It depends on our wisdom, our freedom from illusion and prejudices, our readiness to work, to practice intelligent austerity, and on our kindness and our breadth as human beings. That wisdom must be manifested in a genuine rapprochement among the countries of the First, Second, and the Third worlds; in overcoming disunity in the name of man and his rights. The future of intelligence, of scientific prediction and progress -- the future of the common weal -- must be realized."
